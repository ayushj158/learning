# The Full Answer — Prioritisation Framework + Managing 3 Features

## The Framework Answer

*"I managed three vertically sliced feature teams at Lloyds — each owning a distinct product within the commercial savings portfolio: Instant Access, Fixed Term Deposits, and Notice Accounts. Each team had its own product owner, and I aligned at programme level with a senior PO who held the overall transformation roadmap.*

*The way I think about prioritisation across teams is through three filters.*

*First, business impact — what is the cost of delay? In a regulated financial services environment this isn't abstract. A delay on Instant Access has a direct impact on account opening conversion and savings book growth. A delay on a regulatory change has a compliance and FCA implications. These are not equivalent and I don't treat them as equivalent.*

*Second, dependency depth — is this blocking one engineer or one team or the entire programme? A blocker that sits on the critical path of a C-suite committed go-live gets my attention immediately. A blocker that affects one team's velocity in a non-critical sprint gets triaged differently.*

*Third, reversibility — can we course-correct this decision later or is it a one-way door? Architecture decisions, platform choices, integration patterns — these are one-way doors. Sprint scope and sequencing are two-way doors. I apply much more scrutiny to the former.*

*My practical operating model across three teams was structured but not bureaucratic. I ran a weekly programme-level alignment with all three team leads and the programme PO — thirty minutes, focused on cross-team dependencies, blockers, and anything that needed a prioritisation call from me. Within that I had individual one-to-ones with each team lead. And I maintained direct stakeholder relationships with the Lloyds business leads so I was never relying on filtered information when a priority decision needed to be made.*

*The rule I operated by: I don't prioritise tasks, I prioritise outcomes. The question I ask when two things conflict is — what is the highest value thing that only I can unblock right now?"*

## The Scarce Resource STAR

*"The most concrete prioritisation conflict I navigated was a scarce resource problem across two feature teams.*

*We had a senior cloud and platform engineer on the team — one of the few people who had deep expertise in both our Azure infrastructure and our Kafka event streaming setup. Two feature teams needed him simultaneously. The Instant Access team had a critical integration spike that was blocking their sprint — without his input they couldn't validate the architecture decision and the sprint would stall. At the same time the Term Deposits team had a production readiness review coming up and needed his sign-off on the event streaming configuration before they could proceed to release.*

*Both were legitimate. Both were time-sensitive. I couldn't split him effectively across both without him delivering half-quality on each.*

*I made the call in a structured way. I assessed business impact first — the Instant Access sprint blocker was on our critical path to the C-suite committed go-live. The Term Deposits production readiness review had a two-day window of flexibility before it became critical. So the sequencing was clear: Instant Access gets him for two days, Term Deposits gets him immediately after.*

*But I didn't just make the call and move on. I went to the Term Deposits team lead directly, explained the sequencing decision and the reasoning, and asked what we could do to keep their release preparation moving in the interim. We identified two things they could progress independently — config documentation and environment validation — so they weren't completely stalled.*

*I also flagged this upward to the programme PO as a capacity risk signal. One senior engineer being a single point of dependency across two teams is a structural problem, not just a scheduling one. We used it as the trigger to start cross-training a second engineer on that stack so we didn't face the same constraint again.*

*The outcome: Instant Access unblocked, sprint recovered, Term Deposits release proceeded on schedule two days later. And we eliminated the single point of dependency within the next sprint.*"
